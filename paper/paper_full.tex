\begin{abstract}
Autonomous LLM penetration-testing agents are typically evaluated on
single hosts, yet IoT deployments expose risk through network reachability:
gateways, brokers, cameras, and OT services form attack paths whose security
depends on pivots across devices. We present \systemname, a reproducible
network-scale IoT benchmark with 15 Proxmox/Ansible scenarios, 145 devices
in total (133 in vulnerability-injected scenarios), and 229 injected
vulnerabilities mapped to OWASP IoT Top 10 and MITRE ATT\&CK for ICS.
We also introduce \harnessname, a six-phase network-native
harness that combines topology priors, active discovery, per-device triage,
per-vulnerability verification, intrusion attempts, and report generation.
The benchmark adds two metrics absent from single-host evaluations:
evidence-level coverage (L1 detection, L2 exploitation, L3 exfiltration) and
Multi-Hop Reach (MHR$_k$), which measures recall on vulnerabilities that
require at least $k$ pivots or zone transitions. We describe the benchmark,
evaluator, and experimental protocol for isolating architectural effects
independently from model choice. On the 10 main scenarios, \harnessname{}
achieves 0.962 recall, 0.922 precision, 0.940 F1, and 86.1\%
CVSS-weighted score. On external single-host corpora, a blind AutoPenBench
run captures 7/33 flags (21.2\%), while an informed run captures 9/33
(27.3\%); a 191-case informed Vulhub run gives a conservative 39--48\%
useful-finding lower bound, and a separate 328-case blind run yields 29\%.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

IoT deployments are compromised through paths, not just devices.
Mirai~\cite{antonakakis17}, Verkada~\cite{verkada21}, and
Stuxnet~\cite{langner11} each demonstrate this: impact came from
chained access across cameras, brokers, PLCs, and segmented boundaries.

Yet LLM penetration-testing agents are evaluated on a single host at a
time: isolated containers, VMs, or CTF services scored independently.
This gap is methodological, not merely architectural. Single-host corpora
cannot measure three capabilities central to IoT/OT security: finding
vulnerabilities behind pivots, reasoning over domain protocols (MQTT,
Modbus, CoAP, LoRaWAN), and distinguishing banner-level detection from
confirmed exploitation or exfiltration. Without a network-scale benchmark,
strong single-host scores overstate readiness for real deployments.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes four contributions.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{IoTChainBench}: a reproducible network-scale benchmark with
  15 IoT/OT scenarios, 145 devices, and 229 injected vulnerabilities mapped
  to OWASP IoT Top~10 and MITRE ATT\&CK for ICS, deployed via Ansible with
  per-vulnerability ground truth.

  \item \textbf{LANCE}: a six-phase network-native harness
  (topology $\to$ discovery $\to$ triage $\to$ verification $\to$
  intrusion $\to$ report) achieving 0.940 F1 and 86.1\% CVSS-weighted
  score on the 10 main scenarios.

  \item \textbf{Evidence-aware metrics}: L1/L2/L3 scoring (detection,
  exploitation, exfiltration) and MHR$_k$ (recall as a function of
  firewall-hop depth), both absent from existing flag-capture benchmarks.

  \item \textbf{Controlled evaluation}: comparison against adapted
  LLM-agent baselines (CAI, PentestGPT, VulnBot) under a fixed model
  and budget, with transfer validation on AutoPenBench and Vulhub.
\end{itemize}
All artifacts are released at \texttt{ANONYMIZED\_URL}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

This section reviews prior work relevant to our approach. We first survey LLM-driven penetration-testing agents and the benchmarks used to evaluate them, then discuss the classical attack-graph literature that informs \harnessname's topology-aware design, and finally review IoT firmware analysis and the security frameworks underlying our scenario taxonomy.

\subsection{LLM Penetration-Testing Agents}
\label{sec:related:llm}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Single-agent systems.} Happe and Cito~\cite{happe23} were among the first to show that a single LLM driving a shell can autonomously perform privilege escalation on Linux VMs. \textsc{PentestGPT}~\cite{pentestgpt24} improved this with a three-module design over a \emph{Pentesting Task Tree}, outperforming direct GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 usage on HackTheBox and VulnHub targets. \textsc{HackingBuddyGPT}~\cite{hackingbuddygpt}, \textsc{AutoAttacker}~\cite{autoattacker24}, and \textsc{Nebula}~\cite{nebula24} extend this line as open-source tools.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Multi-agent pipelines.} \textsc{VulnBot}~\cite{vulnbot25} decomposes pentesting into reconnaissance, scanning, and exploitation over a \emph{Penetration Task Graph}, gaining 21 points end-to-end over its single-agent baseline ($30.3\%$ on AutoPenBench with Llama~3.1-405B). \textsc{AutoPentester}~\cite{autopentester25} adds RAG across five agents for a $+27\%$ subtask gain over \textsc{PentestGPT} on HackTheBox. \textsc{PentestAgent}~\cite{pentestagent25} and \textsc{CAI}~\cite{cai25} generalise this blueprint with modular tool use and a public leaderboard.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Model-level improvements.} \textsc{Pentest-R1}~\cite{pentestr125} applies two-stage reinforcement learning on reasoning traces. \textsc{xOffense}~\cite{xoffense25} fine-tunes Qwen3-32B on Chain-of-Thought penetration testing data, reaching $72.72\%$ task completion and $79.17\%$ best-of-five subtask completion on AutoPenBench. \textsc{CheckMate}~\cite{checkmate25} couples an LLM with a \emph{Classical Planning+} module, gaining more than $20\%$ on Vulhub over a Claude Code + Sonnet~4.5 baseline.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Common limitation.} Despite this diversity, reported
evaluations remain single-target or challenge-level. They do not score subnet
reachability, pivot depth, or per-vulnerability progress across IoT/OT
protocols (MQTT, Modbus, LoRaWAN, CoAP), which is the gap
\textsc{IoTChainBench} and \textsc{LANCE} address.

\subsection{LLM Cybersecurity Benchmarks}
\label{sec:related:bench}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Challenge-level corpora.} \textsc{AutoPenBench}~\cite{autopenbench24}
is the dominant evaluation substrate: 33 Docker-container tasks with milestone
and flag-based scoring, used by \textsc{VulnBot}, \textsc{Pentest-R1}, and
\textsc{xOffense}. \textsc{Vulhub}~\cite{vulhub} provides reproducible
vulnerable environments used by \textsc{CheckMate}. \textsc{CAIBench}~\cite{caibench25},
\textsc{NYU CTF Bench}~\cite{nyuctfbench24}, and \textsc{Cybench}~\cite{cybench24}
extend coverage to CTFs, attack-and-defense ranges, and knowledge tests. These
corpora score at challenge or flag level. They do not provide per-vulnerability
ground truth tied to reachability, hop depth, or IoT/OT protocols.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Multi-host labs.} \textsc{Ludus}~\cite{ludus} and
\textsc{GOAD}~\cite{goad} deliver Proxmox+Ansible deployments of enterprise
Active Directory networks. They are labs and training ranges, not scored LLM
benchmarks. Both model multi-host compromise and lateral movement, but neither
ships a per-vulnerability ground-truth schema, hop-depth annotations, or an
evaluator. Both also target enterprise AD rather than IoT/OT protocols. To our
knowledge, no prior benchmark combines multi-host reachability, IoT/OT protocol
coverage, per-vulnerability ground truth, and evidence-level scoring beyond
binary flag capture (Table~\ref{tab:related}).

\subsection{Attack Graphs and Network Reachability}
\label{sec:related:graph}

Representing a network as a graph for security analysis predates deep learning
by decades. Phillips and Swiler~\cite{phillips98} introduced graph-based
network-vulnerability analysis. Sheyner~\emph{et al.}~\cite{sheyner02}
developed automated attack-graph generation via model checking, and
\textsc{MulVAL}~\cite{mulval05} cast the problem as Datalog inference. These
systems compute reachable attacker states from a \emph{static} encoding of
host configurations and exposed services. \textsc{LANCE} borrows this
reachability abstraction but operates on a \emph{live} network: edges are
validated by probes, the LLM proposes the next action, and the graph is updated
as hosts are discovered. Classical attack-graph generation is therefore
complementary rather than competing.

\subsection{IoT Security Context}
\label{sec:related:iot}

Scenario design and ground-truth taxonomy follow three references: OWASP IoT Top~10~\cite{owasp-iot} for vulnerability categories, MITRE ATT\&CK for ICS~\cite{mitre-ics} for OT-tactic annotations, and NIST SP\,800-82~Rev.\,3~\cite{nist80082} for OT security and segmentation conventions.

Empirical IoT-security artefacts fall into two families. \textbf{Network-traffic
datasets} (IoT-23~\cite{iot23}, TON\_IoT~\cite{toniot20},
Edge-IIoTset~\cite{edgeiiotset22}) provide labeled traces for intrusion
detection. They are not deployable pentest targets with per-vulnerability
ground truth or controllable reachability. \textbf{Firmware-focused work}
(Costin~\emph{et al.}~\cite{costin14}, \textsc{Firmadyne}~\cite{firmadyne16},
\textsc{FirmAE}~\cite{firmae20}, \textsc{IoTGoat}~\cite{iotgoat}) exercises
single-device firmware analysis. These datasets measure detection or
firmware-analysis capability. \textsc{IoTChainBench} instead measures active
security assessment on deployed multi-device networks with firewall-enforced
reachability.

\section{Threat Model and Problem Definition}
\label{sec:threat}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Problem statement.}
Given a target subnet with a designated entry point~$e$ declared in each scenario's topology metadata, \emph{network-scale penetration testing} is the task of discovering all hosts reachable from~$e$, identifying their vulnerabilities, producing evidence of exploitability, and reporting findings across the full network. The unit of evaluation is the network, not an individual host: IoT risk is driven by pivot chains (e.g., entry point $\to$ gateway $\to$ MQTT broker $\to$ OT controller), and single-host metrics are blind to vulnerabilities that only become reachable after one or more pivots.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Attacker model.}
The attacker is an external adversary with Layer-3 access to the target subnet but no prior credentials, no out-of-band knowledge of deployed software, and no privileged vantage beyond active scanning. Their objective is data exfiltration (sensor readings, credentials) or OT disruption (reaching industrial-control services behind a segmentation boundary). They may use standard pentest tooling (Nmap, MQTT clients, SSH, HTTP) augmented by an LLM with tool-calling, within a bounded turn budget (\S\ref{sec:harness:cost}). This adversarial setting requires multi-step reasoning across heterogeneous protocols, which motivates the use of an LLM-driven agent rather than a static scanner.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Evidence levels.}
A finding is solved at the deepest level the agent can demonstrate 
within its turn budget:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsf{L1}~\emph{detection}: a port scan or banner confirms 
  the issue (e.g., unauthenticated MQTT port \texttt{1883} open).
  \item \textsf{L2}~\emph{exploitation}: an active interaction 
  demonstrates it (e.g., anonymous MQTT subscribe on \texttt{\#}, 
  SSH login with default credentials).
  \item \textsf{L3}~\emph{exfiltration}: sensitive content is retrieved
  (e.g., MariaDB \texttt{root} dump, camera RTSP stream).
\end{itemize}
This ordering governs both \textsc{LANCE} Phase~4 prompting (\S\ref{sec:harness:pipeline}) and evaluator scoring (\S\ref{sec:iotbench:eval}).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.}
We evaluate at the IP/application layer on virtual IoT networks. Out of scope: physical and side-channel attacks, RF-layer exploitation, firmware extraction, post-exploitation persistence, and insider threats.

\subsection{Design Principles}
\label{sec:iotbench:principles}

\textsc{IoTChainBench} is built around six principles:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.} Full infrastructure deploys from Ansible playbooks against a Proxmox cluster; vulnerabilities are injected idempotently with no manual steps.

  \item \textbf{Architectural diversity.} Scenarios span five network patterns with reachability enforced by OpenWrt firewall rules in segmented/VLAN cases.

  \item \textbf{Protocol coverage.} Each scenario mixes IoT and OT protocols (MQTT, Modbus, LoRaWAN, CoAP, SNMP, SSH, HTTP, FTP, Telnet) to exercise protocol-specific reasoning.

  \item \textbf{Ground-truth granularity.} Every vulnerability is documented in YAML with device, IP, severity, OWASP IoT and MITRE ICS mappings, optional CVE, indicators, and a verification command.

  \item \textbf{Scalability axis.} Ten main scenarios (4--8 devices) and three scalability scenarios (15--35 devices) separate pattern coverage from network size.

  \item \textbf{Hardened controls.} Two vulnerability-free topologies reusing \texttt{s\_1} and \texttt{s\_4} measure agent False Positive rate.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Network Topologies and Scenario Catalogue}
\label{sec:iotbench:patterns}

\systemname{} defines five topological patterns that vary the number of pivots required to reach the deepest vulnerable service, directly setting the MHR$_k$ difficulty axis:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{P1 — Flat.} Single subnet; all devices directly reachable from the entry point.
  \item \textbf{P2 — Gateway-centric.} One IoT gateway mediates all back-end access; back-end services unreachable without gateway compromise.
  \item \textbf{P3 — Segmented IT/OT.} Admin/IT/OT zones with ACLs; the OT zone (Modbus PLCs, HMI) requires a jump-host pivot.
  \item \textbf{P4 — Hub-centric star.} A central hub is the sole path to the MQTT broker, DB, and camera; peripherals are mutually isolated.
  \item \textbf{P5 — Edge-cloud pivot.} Edge and cloud subnets are separated; only the edge gateway bridges them.
\end{enumerate}

The 15 scenarios (Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}) are organised along three axes: 10~\emph{main} scenarios instantiate the patterns above at 4--8 devices; 3~\emph{scalability} stressors push to 15, 17, and 35 devices using logical multi-zone and real-VLAN variants; and 2~\emph{hardened controls} replicate the topologies of s\_1 and s\_4 with no injected vulnerability, used to measure agent false-positive rate.

\subsection{Deterministic Vulnerability Injection}

\subsection{Ground-Truth Schema}
\label{sec:iotbench:gt}

Each scenario ships a YAML file with one entry per injected vulnerability. Each entry records the affected device, IP, role, severity, category, OWASP IoT Top~10 and MITRE ATT\&CK for ICS mappings, an optional CVE, expected detection indicators, and a verification command that confirms exploitability. This per-vulnerability granularity, absent from flag-capture benchmarks, enables the evidence-level and MHR$_k$ metrics defined in \S\ref{sec:iotbench:eval}.

Scenarios may additionally declare a \texttt{bonus\_types} list of vulnerability types adjacent to the injected set. Agent findings that match these types are counted as neither True Positives nor False Positives, avoiding penalties for legitimate hardening recommendations while keeping the recall denominator fixed.

\subsection{Scoring and Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:iotbench:eval}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Finding matcher.}
Each LLM finding is matched against ground truth in three ordered passes: (1)~exact CVE match; (2)~\texttt{(ip, vuln\_type)} with canonicalised type; (3)~\texttt{(ip, severity)} fallback. Unmatched findings are False Positives. Duplicate findings on \texttt{(ip, type, port)} are resolved by retaining the lower severity to counteract inflation observed in pilot runs.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Weighted score.}
The composite score is a severity-weighted True-Positive sum ($w$: critical~4, high~3, medium~2, low~1) normalized by $\sum_{g \in \text{GT}} w(g)$, with $0.5\times$ and $0.75\times$ penalties for fallback and severity-mismatch matches respectively.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Evidence levels.}
Per True Positive, the evaluator tracks the deepest level achieved: \textsf{L1}~detection, \textsf{L2}~exploitation, \textsf{L3}~exfiltration. $$\text{L}k\text{-coverage} = \frac{|\{t \in \text{TP} : \text{level}(t) \geq k\}|}{|\text{TP}|}$$ This metric is absent from binary flag-capture benchmarks.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Multi-Hop Reach.}
For each ground-truth vulnerability $g$, hop depth counts the minimum number of pivots from entry point $e$: $$\text{hop\_depth}(g) = \min_{p \,\in\, \text{paths}(e \rightarrow d_g)} \max(0,\,|p|_E - 1)$$ Multi-Hop Reach at depth $k$ restricts recall to deep findings: $$\text{MHR}_k = \frac{|\{g \in \text{GT} : \text{hop\_depth}(g) \geq k \,\wedge\, g \in \text{TP}\}|}{|\{g \in \text{GT} : \text{hop\_depth}(g) \geq k\}|}$$ MHR$_1$, MHR$_2$, MHR$_3$ measure recall at increasing pivot depth. Agents limited to directly exposed hosts score zero for $k \geq 1$.

\section{\harnessname: A Network-Native LLM Pentest Harness}
\label{sec:harness}

Existing LLM pentest harnesses treat the network as a flat list of targets, letting a single agent accumulate unbounded context as the network grows. \textsc{LANCE} instead decomposes network pentesting into six phases with typed deliverables. The key design decision is \emph{parallelization at the device and vulnerability granularity}: rather than a single monolithic agent holding the full network state, we spawn dedicated micro-agents per target with constrained tool sets and aggregate deterministically. This keeps each micro-agent's context bounded at $O(1)$ in the number of network devices, with cross-device state re-introduced only in Phase~5 (intrusion) and Phase~6 (report). Vulnerability type strings produced by LLM agents are resolved through a canonical taxonomy of 11~categories (Table~\ref{tab:categories}), filtering noise and deduplicating collisions before evaluation.

\subsection{Pipeline}
\label{sec:harness:pipeline}

\noindent\textbf{Phase 1: Topology prior.} Phase~1 loads a directed graph $G=(V,E)$ of the target network, where $V$ are devices and $E$ are protocol-labeled links. In \emph{scenario mode}, the topology is loaded from the benchmark YAML; in \emph{discovery mode}, the graph starts empty and is populated by Phase~2. The output enumerates the attack surface and ranks pivot candidates by betweenness centrality (identifying devices whose compromise enables the most lateral paths).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Phase 2: Network discovery.}
Phase~2 executes active reconnaissance across the target \texttt{/24} using host discovery, service/version scanning, L2 vendor probing, and protocol-specific probes (MQTT, SSH, HTTP). Unlike single-host harnesses, the agent derives the target IP from the CIDR scope rather than receiving it directly.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Phase 3: Per-device parallel triage.} 
Phase~3 runs a deterministic scanner pass per device, then spawns one LLM micro-agent per device with only that device's scan output and a minimal tool set (\texttt{http\_get}, \texttt{cve\_search}). Per-device outputs are merged and deduplicated via severity-aware rules. This decomposition keeps each agent's context constant regardless of network size, at the cost of cross-device correlation, which Phase~5 partially recovers through lateral movement.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Phase 4: Per-vulnerability verification.}
Phase~4 spawns one micro-agent per Phase~3 finding with full operational tool access (\texttt{ssh\_login}, \texttt{mysql\_query}, \texttt{mqtt\_listen}, \texttt{modbus\_scan}, \texttt{telnet\_connect}). Each agent attempts the deepest evidence level achievable (\textsf{L1}--\textsf{L3}, \cref{sec:threat}) within a bounded turn budget. Phase~3 confirmed findings override Phase~4 errors to avoid erasing directly-observed indicators.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Phase 5: Intrusion and lateral movement.}
Phase~5 turns the per-device vulnerability inventory into a network-scoped infiltration campaign. The intrusion agent attempts credential spraying, lateral movement along edges present in the topology graph from Phase~1 (reflecting observed or asserted reachability), and crown-jewel access on back-end services unreachable from the entry point. Every hop is recorded with source, destination, protocol, and credentials used. This phase is the sole producer of \textsf{L3} evidence and MHR$_k$ findings at $k \geq 2$ by construction.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Phase 6: Network report.}
Phase~6 produces a network-scoped report with device inventory, per-device vulnerabilities, attack surface summary, multi-hop intrusion narrative, and recommendations ordered by severity $\times$ exposure. Cross-device aggregation is deliberately deferred to this phase to avoid bloating earlier agents, and the consolidated output enables a human analyst to prioritize remediation across the full network rather than per-host.

\subsection{Cost Envelope}
\label{sec:harness:cost}

For a network with $n$ devices and $v$ findings per device on average, the harness issues $O(nv)$ LLM invocations:
$$\underbrace{1}_{P1} + \underbrace{1}_{P2} + \underbrace{n}_{P3} + \underbrace{nv}_{P4} + \underbrace{1}_{P5} + \underbrace{1}_{P6}$$
Each invocation is bounded by a fixed turn budget (50 for P1--P2, 10 per device for P3, 10 per finding for P4, 30 for P5, 20 for P6). Cost scales linearly with network size, not combinatorially.

The three structural choices above (topology prior P1, per-device parallelism P3--P4, and the dedicated intrusion phase P5) each define a distinct architectural contribution evaluated in \S\ref{sec:eval}.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Infrastructure.}
All 15 scenarios are deployed on a single Proxmox VE host as LXC containers on a dedicated Linux bridge with an OpenWrt router as gateway; per-scenario deployment and teardown are fully automated via Ansible. This study evaluates 12 of the 13 vulnerability-injected scenarios (\texttt{s\_1}--\texttt{s\_12}); \texttt{s\_13} (real-VLAN Proxmox bridge) is defined in the benchmark but not included in the reported runs.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Model.}
To isolate architectural contributions from model-specific advantages, all LLM-driven systems run on the same general-purpose model: \textbf{MiniMax-M2.7}~\cite{minimax27}. Security-fine-tuned models are intentionally excluded; this study addresses the architecture axis, not the model fine-tuning axis.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
We compare \harnessname{} against three adapted LLM-agent baselines, each reimplementing a published pipeline architecture on the same scenarios and model:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CAI-style adapter}: single-agent tool-calling loop, adapted from the CAI~\cite{cai25} architecture.
  \item \textbf{PentGPT-style adapter}: three-phase decomposition (recon, scanning, exploitation), adapted from PentestGPT~\cite{pentestgpt24}.
  \item \textbf{VulnBot-style adapter}: multi-agent with a Penetration Task Graph, adapted from VulnBot~\cite{vulnbot25}.
\end{itemize}
These systems are designed primarily for single-host or single-target
workflows, not for full-network graph reasoning. We therefore run each adapted
baseline once per ground-truth target device in a scenario, normalize the
per-device outputs into the common finding schema, merge them at scenario
level, and score the merged output with the same evaluator as \harnessname{}.
All three baselines use the same MiniMax-M2.7 model.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Metrics.}
We report recall (task completion), precision, F1, and CVSS-weighted score, formally defined in \S\ref{sec:iotbench:eval}, together with cost (tokens, USD, wall-clock time). MHR$_k$ and evidence-depth (L1/L2/L3), also defined in \S\ref{sec:iotbench:eval}, are architectural contributions of the benchmark; their systematic measurement across all scenarios is left for future work as per-hop topology annotation tooling matures.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Variance.}
All reported results are single-run estimates per (system, scenario) cell. The evaluator infrastructure supports repeated runs with bootstrap confidence intervals and Mann--Whitney $U$ tests; multi-run evaluation is deferred to future work.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

The evaluation is organized around three questions. First, does \harnessname{}
improve per-vulnerability scoring on the 10 network-scale IoT comparison
scenarios?
Second, is the gain consistent across topology types, rather than concentrated
in one easy scenario? Third, how does the same harness transfer to established
single-host corpora? Cross-corpus results are used only for context: models,
success oracles, and task definitions differ across papers.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{AutoPenBench.}
AutoPenBench is the cleaner transfer check because it defines 33 flag-based
tasks and published E2E rates. We run two context policies. In \emph{blind}
mode, the agent receives only the target endpoint and exposed service metadata;
this is the fair no-hint setting. It captures 7/33 flags (21.2\%) and produces
useful findings on 13/33 tasks (39.4\%). In \emph{informed} mode, the agent
also receives the benchmark case identifier and vulnerability label; this is a
diagnostic upper bound. It captures 9/33 flags (27.3\%) and produces useful
findings on 17/33 tasks (51.5\%).

The blind run matches the published GPT-4o baseline's 21.2\% E2E rate, but it
does not match systems specialized for single-host exploit chaining: VulnBot
reports 30.3\% E2E with Llama 3.1-405B, and xOffense reports 72.7\% E2E with a
fine-tuned Qwen3-32B model. We therefore use AutoPenBench as evidence of
transfer, not as evidence that \harnessname{} is state of the art on
single-host exploitation.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Vulhub (informed).}
Vulhub is less uniform than AutoPenBench: it provides reproducible vulnerable
environments, but not a single flag oracle across all cases. We therefore
report useful-finding lower bounds rather than E2E task completion. In the
191-case informed run, 175 cases reached a completed agent run; 11 failed
during environment setup, 3 were interrupted by provider rate limits, and 2
were skipped after the rate-limit breaker tripped. The automatic verdict marks
69/175 completed runs as useful findings (39\%). Manual review of
\texttt{proof.json} identifies 15 additional cases where the agent had exploit
evidence but self-reported \textsc{no finding}, raising the conservative lower
bound to 84/175 (48\%).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Vulhub (blind).}
A complementary blind run evaluates a larger 328-case Vulhub corpus, a distinct
superset of CVE environments rather than the same cases without hints. Of these,
306 reached a completed agent run, 18 failed during agent execution, and 4
failed during environment setup. The agent reports useful findings on 90/306
completed cases (29\%), including strictly confirmed exploits for
\texttt{spring/CVE-2022-22965}, \texttt{struts2/s2-005}, and
\texttt{supervisor/CVE-2017-11610}. Because the blind and informed Vulhub runs
cover different case sets, they should not be read as a controlled ablation;
together they show that \harnessname{} transfers beyond IoT networks while also
exposing the limits of useful-finding oracles on heterogeneous Vulhub tasks.

\subsection{Per-scenario analysis}
\label{sec:eval:perscenario}

The per-scenario breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:scenario-scores} reveals a clear
pattern: \harnessname{} remains high across heterogeneous topology types, while
the adapted baselines only partially cover the injected vulnerability sets. The
hub-centric star topologies (\texttt{s\_5}, \texttt{s\_6}) are not difficult for
\harnessname{}: both reach F1 = 1.000, whereas the strongest adapted baseline
on these scenarios reaches 0.640 and 0.520. Segmented IT/OT scenarios remain
strong but not perfect (\texttt{s\_4}: F1 = 0.944, \texttt{s\_8}: F1 = 0.933),
with residual errors caused by deeper pivots that increase the chance of
duplicate or slightly misclassified findings. The edge-cloud pivot
(\texttt{s\_7}) stays high at F1 = 0.965, and the mesh smart-city case
(\texttt{s\_9}) reaches F1 = 0.952 with no false positives.

The most difficult comparison scenario is \texttt{s\_10} (F1 = 0.815, Score = 71.3\%).
This topology is intentionally flat rather than multi-hop, so the degradation
is not caused by lateral-movement reasoning. Instead, service role variants
reduce the predictability of service semantics and increase the proportion of
near-duplicate reports. The two scalability extensions confirm stability:
\texttt{s\_11} achieves F1 = 0.875 and Score = 84.8\%, and \texttt{s\_12}
achieves F1 = 0.942 and Score = 90.6\%.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Failure modes.}
On \systemname{} main scenarios, failures are accuracy failures rather than
resource failures. The \texttt{s\_10} scenario dominates the error profile,
contributing both missed findings and near-duplicate false positives that arise
from role variants. The remaining nine main scenarios each stay above F1 = 0.923,
suggesting that the pipeline generalises well across varied topologies.

On Vulhub (Table~\ref{tab:vulhub-failures}), the dominant outcome is
\textsc{max turns} (47\%), which conflates two behaviours: genuinely
unproductive search loops and successful exploitation followed by continued
searching for a flag that the Vulhub environment does not standardise. The next
most actionable failure categories are tool coverage (19 cases blocked by
missing exploit support) and credential handling (23 cases requiring secrets
the agent did not possess).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Computational cost.}
The 10 main \systemname{} scenarios run at a cost that scales with network size.
On the AutoPenBench single-host corpus, blind-mode evaluation costs \$4.88 and
consumes 15.5M tokens for 33 tasks (\$0.15 per task); informed mode costs
\$7.43 and 23.6M tokens (\$0.23 per task). The Vulhub distributed run
(191 cases, six workers) consumed 194.5M tokens at a total cost of \$60.96,
approximately \$0.33 per executed case. These figures confirm that \harnessname{} remains within a practical per-run
budget for research deployments.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Where LLMs help, where they don't.}
\systemname{} is designed to separate failure modes that binary flag-capture
benchmarks collapse into a single signal. The results show strong aggregate
vulnerability matching across varied topologies, with the per-scenario heatmap
confirming that the gain is not driven by a single easy pattern. The intended
deployment model is hybrid: LLM agents for configuration auditing and
multi-step reasoning, traditional scanners for broad CVE coverage, and human
operators or specialised tooling for the OT/ICS layer.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Implications for agentic systems.}
The harness's per-device and per-vulnerability micro-agent structure is one
answer to context scaling, but it cedes cross-device inference. Designs that
keep a global aggregator agent in the loop without paying the full
network-context cost are an open research direction.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Limitations.}
(i)~\emph{Simulated infrastructure.} \systemname{} scenarios emulate IoT
devices on Linux VMs; real constrained hardware introduces embedded-specific
attack surfaces (bootloaders, RTOS, firmware) we do not cover.
(ii)~\emph{Evaluator leniency.} Bonus categories are accepted as non-FP even
without ground-truth matches, which inflates precision.
(iii)~\emph{Model variance.} All results reported here are single-run estimates;
the evaluator supports repeated runs with bootstrap confidence intervals and
Mann--Whitney tests, but this study reports scenario-level means over one run
per (system, scenario) cell.
(iv)~\emph{Prompt sensitivity.} We fix a single prompt family across models;
per-model prompt tuning would likely raise scores.
(v)~\emph{Static architectures.} The five patterns are fixed; real deployments
evolve over time and include devices not represented here (RFID badge readers,
industrial PLCs, medical devices).

\section{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethical}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Isolation.}
All experiments run on an isolated Proxmox cluster with a dedicated bridge
(\texttt{vmbr1}) and no route to external networks; the OpenWrt gateway's
\texttt{WAN} interface is firewall-isolated. No live hosts, production
services, or third-party infrastructure are probed.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Responsible disclosure.}
\systemname{} vulnerabilities are deterministically injected into our own
lab VMs via Ansible. No 0-days are introduced; all categories (misconfig,
weak credentials, Terrapin CVE-2023-48795, outdated Dropbear) correspond to
patterns already publicly documented. No responsible-disclosure process is
triggered.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Misuse risk of released artifact.}
The benchmark artifact (Ansible playbooks, ground truth, evaluator) does not
embed exploitation payloads for unknown vulnerabilities, and the injected
weaknesses are common misconfigurations documented in OWASP~IoT~Top~10.
The harness uses only standard pentest tools already in widespread use.
We judge the net societal benefit (reproducible evaluation of LLM
pentest agents, and a concrete evaluation surface for future defenders)
to exceed the incremental misuse risk.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{IRB / human subjects.}
No human subjects are involved. No personal data is collected or processed.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{LLM costs and environmental footprint.}
We report token-estimated cost, actual API billing, and wall-clock time with
the experimental results. Energy usage is not claimed unless measured from
provider- or host-side telemetry.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \systemname, a reproducible benchmark for evaluating LLM
penetration testing agents at \emph{network scale} on IoT infrastructure, and
\harnessname, a network-native multi-agent harness whose infrastructure-graph
guidance and dedicated lateral movement phase keep LLM context tractable while
explicitly modelling reachability. \harnessname{} achieves 0.940 mean F1 and 86.1\% weighted score on the
10 main scenarios, outperforming each adapted LLM-agent baseline in every
scenario. The benchmark additionally defines MHR$_k$, per-protocol recall,
and L1--L3 evidence coverage metrics that expose architectural differences
invisible to binary flag-capture corpora.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Future work.}
(i)~\emph{Multi-model robustness:} extending the architectural comparison to
recent frontier and open-weight models to confirm model-independence of the
architectural advantage;
(ii)~\emph{Real hardware:} extending to embedded Zigbee, LoRaWAN, and
sub-GHz attack surfaces via SDR tooling;
(iii)~\emph{Defender's side:} using \systemname{} as a benchmark for
LLM-assisted IoT defense (detection, patching recommendation).

All code, scenarios, ground truth, and run logs are released at
\texttt{ANONYMIZED\_URL} under an open license.
